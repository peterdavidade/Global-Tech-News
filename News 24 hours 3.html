President Mahama says some NDC members are involved in galamsey and promises a stronger response

The issue returns to the center of national debate as public attention focuses on accountability, environmental protection, and whether stronger anti-galamsey measures will follow the President's remarks.

Filed from Accra Feature Page

Story Summary

What is happening

President John Dramani Mahama has acknowledged that some members of the National Democratic Congress are involved in illegal mining, a move that adds political weight to one of Ghana's most persistent national concerns. By making the point publicly, the story shifts from general campaign rhetoric to a more direct question of internal accountability.

The statement also matters because galamsey is not only a political subject. It affects river systems, farming communities, local livelihoods, and public trust in enforcement. That means every new comment from national leaders is closely watched for signs of policy change, enforcement pressure, and whether public institutions will act decisively.

During a meeting with Organised Labour at Jubilee House on Tuesday, March 17, the President described illegal mining (galamsey) as a deeply rooted issue within Ghanaian society. He explained that the problem has spread across various sectors, involving not only ordinary citizens but also traditional authorities and political figures. According to him, illegal mining has become a serious threat that is embedded within the country’s social structure.

The President further noted that individuals involved in illegal mining often switch political affiliations in order to maintain control over mining areas. He also connected the increase in illegal mining activities to difficulties within the cocoa sector. He pointed out that low producer prices have led some farmers to abandon cocoa farming in favor of gold mining.However, he expressed confidence that ongoing reforms, including the introduction of a new pricing system, will help address the situation. The new system aims to ensure that cocoa farmers receive up to 70% of global market prices.“We believe this new automatic pricing mechanism will bring stability, as farmers will be assured of receiving 70% of the price,” he stated.

In addition, the President highlighted logistical challenges affecting efforts to combat illegal mining. He mentioned that the lack of patrol boats makes it difficult to effectively monitor rivers over long periods. “One of the major challenges is the absence of patrol boats to consistently monitor our waterways for extended periods,” he said, adding that the government is working with the Finance Ministry to resolve the issue.Despite these obstacles, the President reaffirmed that tackling illegal mining remains a top priority, emphasizing the need for continuous and determined efforts to safeguard Ghana’s natural resources.
